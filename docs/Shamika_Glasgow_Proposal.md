## **Preliminary Proposal Template**

**Project Title:**	AI-Assisted Emergency Department Triage Optimisation 

**Student Name:**	Shamika Glasgow  
	

**Statement of the Problem:**

Emergency department clinicians at Mercer General Hospital face variability in triage decision-making, which can lead to inconsistent patient prioritisation. Research conducted in Caribbean emergency departments has identified key organisational challenges including nursing shortages, resource constraints, and complex patient flow processes that compound decision-making difficulties in these settings [(1)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?GUqBnV). Existing AI-assisted triage models demonstrate strong performance but are often trained on limited datasets and lack validation across diverse populations. This project proposes a 12-week pilot to evaluate whether a machine learning model trained on local data can improve triage consistency and reduce variability in clinical decision-making. 

**Previous Work:**

Existing literature demonstrates that machine learning models can significantly improve prediction accuracy and decision-making in emergency department triage. Studies such as Tschoellitsch et al. and Chen et al. [(2,3)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?VbLMJ1) show that machine learning models can accurately predict patient outcomes, including hospital admission and the need for intensive care, with strong performance metrics such as high AUC values.

Additionally, advancements in artificial intelligence, including large language models, have shown potential to enhance clinical reasoning and information-gathering processes, which are critical to effective triage [(4)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?EGECuc). Review studies further highlight the benefits of AI systems in improving efficiency, consistency, and resource allocation in emergency settings [(5,6)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?PTe1DB).

However, for AI-assisted triage systems to be effectively adopted in clinical practice, clinician trust must also be considered. Research on explainable AI suggests that the degree to which clinicians trust AI recommendations depends heavily on the clarity and quality of explanations provided, with poorly designed explanations risking both overtrust and undertrust in AI systems [(7)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?TFSVID). This has direct implications for implementation in emergency settings, where clinician confidence in AI outputs is essential for consistent use.

Despite these promising developments, most existing research relies on retrospective or simulated datasets and lacks real-world validation, particularly in resource-constrained environments such as Caribbean healthcare systems [(1)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?I4n1J4). This limits the generalisability and practical implementation of these models in diverse clinical contexts.

**Proposed Solution:**

This project proposes the development and evaluation of a machine learning model trained on locally sourced emergency department data from Mercer General Hospital. By using real-world clinical data, the model aims to improve the consistency and accuracy of triage decision-making while addressing the limitations of existing systems.

The proposed solution includes training a predictive model to assist in patient prioritisation and evaluating its performance in reducing variability among clinicians. A 12-week pilot study will be conducted to assess whether the model improves triage consistency, supports clinical decision-making, and is suitable for implementation in a resource-constrained Caribbean setting. Ensuring the model produces clear, interpretable outputs will also be a key design consideration, given evidence that explainability is critical to clinician trust and adoption [(7)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?8QanYb).

This approach directly addresses the identified gap in the literature by focusing on real-world validation, local data relevance, and practical applicability in diverse healthcare environments.
